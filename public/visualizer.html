SECTION 1 · THE PROBLEM

[ What Are We Solving? ]

A rocket missile is launched from the ground at v₀ = 20 m/s at an angle θ above the horizontal. We want to know: how far does it travel before hitting the ground? And which angle θ gives the maximum range? Then — using the Saturn V as a real example — how does a rocket actually behave when thrust, drag, and changing mass are included?

📐
TWO MODELS
Model 1 is a clean parabola (no air, no thrust). Model 2 is the real Saturn V: variable mass, real thrust, atmospheric drag.
🎯
THREE OUTPUTS
Fig 3: trajectory fan (all angles). Fig 4: range vs angle table. Fig 5: Saturn V height, velocity and acceleration vs time.
⚙️
THE TOOL
MATLAB's for-loop stepping through time, computing positions analytically (Part 1) or numerically via Euler's method (Part 2).
SECTION 2 · PART 1

[ The Parabolic Projectile Model ]

Start simple. Ignore drag and thrust entirely — the only force is gravity pulling straight down. This gives us a problem we can solve with pure algebra.

The physics intuition: In the horizontal direction, nothing pushes or pulls the rocket — so it moves at constant speed forever. In the vertical direction, gravity decelerates it, stops it, then pulls it back down. That combination of "constant horizontal" + "gravity-curved vertical" is exactly what produces a parabola.

  • Atmospheric air has no effect (no drag)
  • No thrust after launch (purely ballistic)
  • Launched from ground level: x₀ = 0, y₀ = 0
  • Earth's curvature is ignored
  • Mass variation (fuel burn) — NOT modelled here
  • Variable gravity with altitude — NOT modelled here

THE GOVERNING EQUATIONS

VELOCITY COMPONENTS (Eq. 3 & 4) vx₀ = v₀ · cos(θ) ← horizontal velocity (constant, no force acts) vy₀ = v₀ · sin(θ) ← initial vertical velocity
θ is the launch angle above the horizontal. v₀ = 20 m/s.
POSITION AS A FUNCTION OF TIME (Eq. 1 & 2) y(t) = vy₀·t − ½ · g · t² ← height: rises then falls (g = 9.81 m/s²) x(t) = vx₀·t ← horizontal distance: linear in time
The ½gt² term is what makes this a parabola. At t=0 both are zero (ground level launch).
TIME OF FLIGHT (Eq. 5 & 6) Set y(t) = 0 → t₂ = −2·vy₀ / g ← when does it hit the ground? With v₀=20, θ=45°: t₂ = 2·(20·sin45°)/9.81 ≈ 2.887 s
Factoring out t: 0 = t·(v_y₀ − ½gt), so t=0 (launch) or t₂ = −2v_y₀/g (landing).
RANGE FORMULA + MAXIMUM RANGE CONDITION Range = x(t₂) = vx₀ · t₂ = v₀·cos(θ) · (2·v₀·sin(θ)/g) = v₀² · sin(2θ) / g Maximum when sin(2θ) = 1 → 2θ = 90° → θ = 45° Max range = 20² / 9.81 = 40.7747 m ← matches MATLAB output exactly
💡
WHY 45° IS THE MAGIC ANGLE
Range = v₀²·sin(2θ)/g. The only thing that varies with angle is sin(2θ). sin is maximized at 90°, so 2θ=90° → θ=45°. At this angle, horizontal and vertical components are perfectly balanced: equal time in the air AND equal horizontal speed. Any other angle sacrifices one for the other.

SECTION 3 · PART 2 — REAL-LIFE APPLICATION

[ The Saturn V Rocket — ODE Model ]

The parabola model is too simple for a real rocket. The Saturn V (Apollo 11) has: burning fuel that changes its mass every second, enormous thrust for 150 seconds, and air drag that slows it down. None of these can be represented by a simple formula — we need differential equations.

🚀
VARIABLE MASS
Saturn V starts at 2,900,000 kg. Fuel: 2,150,000 kg. After 150 s of burn, only 750,000 kg of structure remains.
🔥
CONSTANT THRUST
Five F-1 engines produce F = 34,000,000 N for exactly 150 seconds, then cut off.
💨
ATMOSPHERIC DRAG
Drag D = Cd · 0.6, where Cd = 0.6 (constant simplified model). Paper notes drag could be altitude-dependent.

THE FORCE BALANCE — NEWTON'S SECOND LAW

For a rocket flying straight up, three forces act on it simultaneously: Thrust (up), Weight (down), Drag (down, opposing motion). Newton's 2nd law F=ma becomes:

FORCE BALANCE (Eq. 10 & 12) Simple constant-mass case: M·a = F − M·g Full variable model: M(t)·a(t) = F(t) − D(t,v,h) − M(t)·g ← Eq. 12
F(t) = 34×10⁶ N when 0 < t < 150 s, else 0. D is drag. M(t) is decreasing mass.
INSTANTANEOUS ACCELERATION (Eq. 13) a(t) = [ F(t) − D(t,v,h) − M(t)·g ] / M(t) During burn (0 < t < 150s), paper uses M = 0.75×10⁶ kg, F = 34×10⁶ N: a = (34×10⁶ − 0.36 − 0.75×10⁶ · 9.81) / 0.75×10⁶ ≈ 35.52 m/s²
No closed-form solution: a, v, h are all coupled and changing every timestep. Must solve numerically.

EULER'S METHOD — THE NUMERICAL SOLUTION

The key idea: if you know where you are, how fast you're going, and your acceleration RIGHT NOW, you can predict where you'll be a tiny time step Δt later. Repeat thousands of times = full trajectory.

FORWARD DIFFERENCING — THE EULER STEP (Eq. 7, 8, 9) aₖ = dv/dt ≈ (vk+1 − vₖ) / Δt ← rearrange to find next v vk+1 = vₖ + Δt · aₖ ← new velocity (Eq. 8) hk+1 = hₖ + Δt · vₖ ← new height (Eq. 9)
1
Set initial conditions
Rocket starts at ground level, at rest. v=0, h=0, t=0.
v = 0; h = 0; tstart = 0; dt = 0.1; tstop = 300;
2
At each timestep: compute acceleration
Get current F(t), M(t), D(t,h,v). Apply Newton's 2nd law → a.
a = GetAcceleration(t, h, v); // a = (F - D - M*g) / M
3
Update height and velocity
Height is updated FIRST (using current velocity), then velocity is updated using current acceleration.
h = h + dt * v; // update height first v = v + dt * a; // then update velocity
4
Store values, repeat for all t up to 300 s
Capture A(k), V(k), H(k), T(k) into vectors for plotting. Step forward: t += 0.1.
T[k]=t; H[k]=h; V[k]=v; A[k]=a; k++;
⚡
WHY DOES VELOCITY START SLIGHTLY NEGATIVE?
In the paper's model, thrust only activates for t > 0. At exactly t=0, F=0, so the only force is gravity pulling the rocket down. For one tiny step (0.1 s), gravity decelerates it: v = 0 + 0.1×(−9.81) = −0.981 m/s. Then at t=0.1 s, thrust kicks in at full 34MN and immediately overpowers gravity (a ≈ +35.5 m/s²). This explains the brief negative dip visible in Fig. 5's velocity graph.

CONCLUSION

[ What This Paper Teaches ]

📈
CLOSED-FORM vs NUMERICAL
Simple problems (no drag, constant mass) have exact formulas. Complex real-world problems require numerical stepping — Euler's method is the simplest approach.
⚖️
45° IS UNIVERSAL
Under no-drag conditions, 45° always maximizes range. This comes directly from sin(2θ) reaching its maximum of 1 at θ=45°. A pure mathematical result.
🔗
ODEs ARE COUPLED
Height, velocity and acceleration are linked: a = dv/dt, v = dh/dt. Change one and you change them all. Euler decouples them by stepping through tiny Δt intervals.
🏗️
ALGORITHM THINKING
The paper demonstrates 5-step computational problem solving: define the problem → identify I/O → design algorithm → implement in MATLAB → test and verify.
